An Australian Election Forecaster

Greens Balance Of Power seems likely

Predicting the senate is very difficult, because (a) there are no polls for the senate, and (b) senate preference flows are near impossible to predict. But here's our best guess for what the senate looks like.

Labor:

29 Seats

Greens:

12 Seats

ONP:

1 Seat

IND:

2 Seats

Coalition:

32 Seats

No one is going to get a majority. It's essentially impossible that any party get a majority in the Australian senate. That's because senate seats are allocated proportional to the vote of each party in each state. Neither of the major parties are polling above 50% which would be needed for a senate majority. Therefore we need to talk about the balance of power.

If we assume that Labor and the Liberals are on opposite sides of a motion, the balance of power is controlled by the party or parties that can decide the outcome the motion. The model currently predicts the Greens win 6 seats giving them a total of 12 seats. If the Greens vote with Labor (or the Liberals) then combined they will have more than the 38 seats needed for a senate majority.

Steven Birmingham or Penny Wong? The leader of the government in the senate is not determined by the actual senate results. Instead, if the Coalition are returned to power then Birmingham will remain the leader of the house, even if the Greens and Labor commanded a senate majority. The same is true if Labor win: Penny Wong would become leader of the government in the senate, even if Labor weren't the largest party in the senate?

This model only attempts to find the most likely outcome for the senate result. Making an accurate prediction is hard! For example, the model is currently predicting that the liberals win three senate seats in NSW, but only two in QLD. That could happen but that seems distinctly unlikely. So take these values as qualitative rather than predictive.

The Australian senate is unfair. The ACT only has two senators, neither of which are up for election this year. Meanwhile, Tasmania has twelve senators and six are up for re-election. This is despite the fact that they have similar populations (430,000 vs 540,000). The six times over-represenation isn't the worst of it. Because ACT and NT senators are elected at the same time, it is almost guaranteed that Labor will receive one senator and the Coalition the other. This denies the territories democratic choice, making the senate an unrepresentative body.
